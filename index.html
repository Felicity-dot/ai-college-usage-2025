How Common Is College AI in 2025?

AI use among college students has surged dramatically in recent years. According to a 2024 survey by Campus Technology and the Digital Education Council, 86% of students globally use AI in their studies (Campus Technology). In the U.S., awareness and use are even higher. At the University of Michigan, 98.5% of students knew about ChatGPT, and 58.1% had used it by early 2024 (Michigan Daily).

According to Axios, 90% of students had already used ChatGPT for assignments by January 2023. This shift reveals a deeper transformation: AI assistants have become as fundamental as search engines or word processors.

How Students Use Writing and Paper AI to Improve Academic Performance

AI supports a wide range of academic tasks:

  • Research and information search: 69% of students use AI Assistants to find and summarize course-related information (Campus Technology).
  • Writing and editing: 42% rely on Writing AI for grammar and style checks, while 24% use it to generate first drafts.

Which Students Use College AI the Most

AI adoption varies across academic groups. Upper-year students use AI Assistants more frequently than freshmen, likely due to heavier workloads (Michigan Daily).

STEM majors rely on AI coding tools, while humanities students favor writing and Paper AI for essays and research (Campbell University).

International students and those with learning differences use AI Assistants for language support and accessibility (eScholarship).

Institutional differences also matter: at high-tuition colleges, 79.4% of students use ChatGPT, compared to 53.9% at lower-tuition schools (Michigan Daily).

How Colleges Are Responding to AI Adoption

As student use of AI surges, colleges are struggling to keep pace with this rapid transformation. A 2024 EDUCAUSE poll found that only 33% of colleges provide official AI tools, and fewer than 10% have integrated them into learning platforms. Even more striking, only 8% of institutions had student AI literacy programs by early 2024, though many plan to introduce such initiatives soon.

This lag in institutional readiness has led to widespread confusion around AI policies. A survey by Inside Higher Ed found that approximately 31% of U.S. undergraduates were uncertain whether or when AI use was permitted in their coursework, and only 16% reported that their schools had published clear AI-use guidelines. On a broader scale, 81% of college presidents acknowledged that their institutions had yet to implement formal AI policies. This policy vacuum often leaves faculty members to make autonomous decisions regarding AI use, resulting in inconsistent rules even within the same campus.

Despite the gaps, progress is underway. Many campuses are piloting AI training programs and ethics workshops. Campus Technology reports that 73% of students support faculty AI training, and 72% want hands-on student instruction. Moreover, a majority (69%) of respondents in Chegg‚Äôs 2025 global survey expressed that their colleges should provide tailored generative AI tools to support learning (Chegg Investor Relations). In response, many campuses are expanding AI literacy and ethics courses, while some institutions have launched dedicated AI research centers and institutes to explore AI‚Äôs educational impact.

Student Perspectives on AI Assistants: Benefits and Concerns

Students approach AI with curiosity, caution, and growing dependence. Over 53% say AI has improved their ability to understand difficult material (Chegg).

For neurodiverse students, AI helps structure assignments and reduce overwhelm (Jisc).

Yet concerns remain. Students cite bias, inaccuracy, and data privacy as top anxieties. 59% of administrators say AI has increased academic dishonesty (Axios).

Still, only 6.8% of students want to remove AI tools entirely. Instead, 71% want a voice in tool selection and clear rules for usage (Campus Technology).

The Skills Debate: Do AI Assistants Harm or Help Student Skills?

Some fear that reliance on AI weakens critical thinking, problem-solving, and writing skills. In response, some programs now emphasize open-ended design challenges and justification of AI-assisted answers (Inside Higher Ed).

One computer science major noted fears of job loss from automation (eScholarship).

Globally, nearly 50% of students feel unprepared for an AI-driven workforce due to lack of training (Campus Technology).
